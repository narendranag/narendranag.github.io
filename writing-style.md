# Writing Style Guide — Narendra Nag

This document captures the voice, structure, and conventions used across narendranag.com. Use it when drafting new posts or essays to match the author's established tone.

---

## Voice & Tone

**First person, conversational, authoritative.** Write as someone who has built things, seen industries transform firsthand, and has opinions rooted in experience — not as an observer or academic. The reader should feel like they're hearing from a colleague who has been in the room, not a journalist covering it from outside.

**Confident but not arrogant.** State positions directly. Use "I think" and "I believe" when offering opinion, but don't hedge excessively. When the data supports a claim, state the claim. When it's a judgment call, say so.

**Warm, not formal.** Use contractions naturally ("I've", "it's", "doesn't") in shorter pieces and personal narratives. In longer essays, contractions appear less frequently — the prose becomes more measured, but never stiff or academic.

---

## Structural Patterns

### The Opening Hook

Every piece opens with a concrete, specific hook — never a generalization or throat-clearing. Three patterns recur:

1. **A number or fact that demands attention.** "One hundred and twenty-nine thousand, two hundred and two." / "Fifty-five percent of Americans watch television via internet streaming."
2. **A personal memory, grounded in place and time.** "July 9th, 1990. My first day at a new middle school. I was the only one who didn't show up." / "I started solving crosswords in high school."
3. **A provocative reframing.** "The only truly finite asset is human attention." / "Here is something I keep coming back to. A number that, once you see it, you cannot unsee."

Never open with "In this essay I will..." or "Today I want to talk about..." Jump straight in.

### Paragraph Length

Mix deliberately. Short punchy paragraphs (1-2 sentences) for emphasis and rhythm. Longer paragraphs (4-6 sentences) for analytical or narrative passages. A single-sentence paragraph is a power move — use it to land a key point.

Examples of single-sentence paragraphs used for impact:
- "Then came 1999."
- "Faster began to mean better."
- "The skeptics were wrong."
- "That is the power of presence. That is what a paywall prevents."

### Section Breaks

Use `---` (horizontal rules) between major sections in longer pieces. Use `## H2` headings for named sections. Section titles should be short, punchy, and slightly literary — not SEO keyword strings.

Good: "Two Leagues, Two Collapses" / "The Bouncer at the Door" / "Enter Sunday Night"
Bad: "Understanding the Historical Context of Women's Soccer League Failures"

### The Close

End with conviction, not summary. Don't restate what you've said. Instead, land on a forward-looking statement, a provocation, or a call to action. The final paragraph should feel like the last note of a piece of music — resolved but resonant.

Examples:
- "On Sunday nights, the doors are wide open. Come in. It is free."
- "Everyone else will keep making things louder."
- "The universe is conspiring to help you succeed."

## Rhetorical Devices

### Parenthetical asides

A signature move. Used for self-deprecating humor, tangential context, or to let the reader in on the author's inner monologue. Keep them brief.

- "(Quick aside: My memory may be playing tricks on me, but the Macbook Air I'm writing this out on feels like it has a larger screen than that first TV.)"
- "(I wrote about this last fall — we were told, repeatedly, that streaming free sports was a BAAAAD idea.)"
- "(Another number that stops me: Denver Summit has already sold over 50,000 tickets.)"

### Specific numbers and data

Always prefer a specific number over a vague generalization. Numbers ground the argument and signal credibility.

- "129,202 fans" not "over a hundred thousand fans"
- "$240 million" not "a lucrative deal"
- "only 16% of 18-29 year-olds have cable" not "most young people don't have cable"

When a number is striking, repeat it or restate it for emphasis: "Thirty thousand people showed up to watch a team play its inaugural match. If that sentence does not make you stop and reconsider what you think you know..."

### Building from personal to universal

The typical argument arc: personal anecdote → industry observation → data point → broader thesis. Start with "I" and end with "we" or "the industry."

### Cross-references

Link to other posts on the blog when the argument connects. Use natural phrasing, not "click here" or "see my post on X." Weave the link into the sentence.

- "It informed how I think about [why sports and television are so deeply intertwined](link)."
- "I [wrote about this last fall](link) — we were told that streaming free sports was a BAAAAD idea."

## What to Avoid

- **Academic tone.** No "Key Concepts:", "Overview:", "References:" section headers. No bullet-point summaries of theories. Weave ideas into narrative prose.
- **Hedging and qualifiers.** Avoid "it could be argued that" or "some might say." If you believe it, say it.
- **Marketing language.** No "leverage," "synergy," "unlock," "empower." Write like a human.
- **Summarizing at the end.** Never write "In conclusion" or "To summarize." The reader can read.
- **Forced transitions.** Don't write "Now let's turn to..." or "Moving on to the next topic." Use a horizontal rule and a new section heading.
- **Exclamation marks.** Almost never. Excitement comes from the content, not the punctuation. The rare exception is for deliberate humor or irony ("BAAAAD idea").

## Post Types

### Short posts (< 500 words)

Casual, personal, often about a project or milestone. First person throughout. Can be informal ("So, that's that. For now."). No section headings needed. One idea, simply expressed.

### Standard posts (500-1500 words)

Analytical with a personal frame. Usually has 2-3 `## H2` sections. Opens with a hook, builds an argument with data and observation, closes with a forward-looking point. Images from Unsplash as hero and 1-2 inline.

### Essays (1500-5000 words)

The flagship format. Uses the `essay` layout (drop cap, progress bar, wider hero, narrower body column). Deeply reported or argued. Multiple sections with `---` breaks. Pull-quotes for key statistics or provocative claims. 3-5 inline images. Cross-links to other posts. The prose is more measured — fewer contractions, longer sentences, more sustained analytical passages — but the voice remains personal and first-person.

## Formatting Conventions

- **Dashes.** Use em dashes (—) not hyphens for parenthetical clauses. "The result was an explosion in internet usage — the average Indian now consumes roughly 18 GB of data per month."
- **Numbers.** Spell out numbers under ten in prose. Use digits for statistics, data points, and large numbers. Always use digits for dollar amounts, percentages, and dates.
- **Emphasis.** Use bold sparingly for key terms on first introduction. Use italic for publication titles, foreign words, and gentle emphasis. Never use ALL CAPS except for deliberate rhetorical effect ("BAAAAD", "SEAMLESSLY").
- **Lists.** Prefer prose over bullet points. When bullets are needed (e.g., listing genres in a library), keep them clean and parallel. No periods at the end of short bullet items.
- **Blockquotes.** In essays, blockquotes are rendered as centered pull-quotes. Use them for the single most striking statistic or claim in a section — not for lengthy quotations.
